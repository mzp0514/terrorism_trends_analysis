State health officials are seeking $ 1.5 million to improve West
Virginia's ability to combat terrorism using chemical weapons.
Carl Berryman, an epidemiologist at the Bureau for Public Health, said
Friday the money would help officials at local health departments
quickly report disease outbreaks by e-mail.  mail.  mail.
And it would improve
diagnostic work at the state lab in Charleston.
Health officials also could improve monitoring of diseases and come up
with plans to be ready for outbreaks of illnesses as well as diagnose
chemical agents such as pesticides, major chemical spills and nerve
gas, he said.
The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention is giving $ 41 million
to states to respond to threats of terrorism using agents such as
anthrax. The Atlanta-based group will notify states of award grants by
Sept. 1.
Berryman played down the threat of bioterrorism in West Virginia.
"The chances of biological attack are less than getting run over by a
bus tonight," he said.
Still five threats of anthrax use that turned out to be hoaxes were
made in West Virginia in the past six months.
Anthrax mostly affects farm animals, but its spores produce a bacteria
that, if inhaled, can cause flulike symptoms and lead to death if not
treated early with antibiotics. Symptoms can develop up to 24 hours
after exposure.  exposure.  exposure.
